This is a teenager

by Alvin Chang

This is a teenager.

(We'll call him Alex.)

It's 1997.

Bill Clinton will start his second term as US president. Men In Black and Titanic will be released in theaters. And humans will land a rover on Mars.

But Alex has other things to worry about.

Alex is a 13-year-old Hispanic kid being raised by his dad and step-mom.

Neither parent has a college degree. Their household income is below the poverty line. And their family's net worth is less than $2,000.

When asked about his mom and dad's parenting style, he said they were not very supportive and not very involved.

In fact, there were many indications that his home environment was pretty bad.

Of the 21 questions he answered about his home and family life, there were 7 answers that raised red flags – way above average.

A year from now, in 1998, a researcher named Vincent Felitti will publish a paper that drastically changes the way we think about Alex – and his childhood.

The research will tell us that these childhood stressors build up and eventually have a big impact on a person's health, earnings, and basically everything else in their life.

Over the next 25 years, researchers will interview Alex regularly. So we'll know how hard his life gets.

He'll be bullied at school.

He'll be held back a few grades.

He'll be the victim of a violent crime.

Many years from now, when he's an adult, Alex will tell the researchers that he struggles with his physical and mental health.

But, for now, Alex is a teenager.

And he's pretty optimistic about his future – just like most teenagers. Let's meet them now.

When a kid turns 18, they're considered an adult. It's an arbitrary age; their brains are still developing, they're still figuring out what they want to do in life, and they often need to learn how to do basic things, like feed themselves.

In 2000, psychologist Jeffrey Arnett published a paper arguing that there's a distinct era between ages 18 to 25 that is neither adolescence or adulthood. He dubbed it "emerging adulthood."

Emerging adulthood is a time of life when many different directions remain possible, when little about the future has been decided for certain, when the scope of independent exploration of life's possibilities is greater for most people than it will be at any other period of the life course. … Emerging adults tend to have a wider scope of possible activities than persons in other age periods because they are less likely to be con- strained by role requirements, and this makes their demographic status unpredictable.

Arnett wasn't the first to think up this idea. He cited German psychoanalyze Erik Erikson, who wrote about the "psychosocial moratorium" that developed countries give to young people. It's a period "during which the young adult through free role experimentation may find a niche in some section of his society."

But lots of teens never get to have a period of emerging adulthood, which tends to happen in college.

Many of them are forced to jump straight into the workforce, or they're in the painful limbo between high school and, well, whatever comes after.

By 2003, we can very clearly see that colleges are more likely to be filled with people who were able to follow the traditional path for success – people who were able to experience childhood, rather than have to deal with so much adult shit.

As a teenager, I didn't understand what my politics were. I vaguely knew that people around me thought liberals were bad; I remember my classmates teasing a history teacher who was a self-identified liberal. But I didn't understand how your opinion on issues like abortion, taxes, and affirmative action were related to one big label like "liberal" and "conservative."

I went to college in New York, and I learned about what it means to be a liberal and a conservative. People around me were liberal – people I thought were good, smart people – so I absorbed their politics. And eventually I ended up reporting on politics.

To be honest, it was miserable. Politics is important, but talking to people who care about politics was completely uninteresting. I could predict their answers, their complaints, their frustrations. When Trump was elected, it only highlighted the political divide between the Red team and Blue team.

It was only after I left newsrooms that I started to re-articulate what I believe and why I believe it.

And, for me, so much of it comes down to the story you see here:

This is a teenager. She has inherited her circumstances, and she has inherited the problem of this world. In the next 25 years, she will experience some terrible things. In fact, most teenagers will go through some traumatic events, because that's a part of life.

I want to live in a world where people have the resources and support to wade through those terrible things, to have a chance to figure out what role they want to play in the world, to have a chance to play and explore and, honestly, mess up.

The gap between that world and reality is my politics. It's why I left newsrooms to teach full-time. It's why I'm drawn to stories like this. And it's TKTKTKTKTK.
