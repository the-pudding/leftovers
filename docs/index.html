This is a teenager

by Alvin Chang

This is a teenager.

(We'll call him Alex.)

It's 1997.

Bill Clinton will start his second term as US president. Titanic will be released in theaters. And humans will land a rover on Mars.

But Alex has other things to worry about.

Alex is a 13-year-old Hispanic kid being raised by his dad and step-mom.

Neither parent has a college degree. Their household income is below the poverty line. And their family's net worth is less than $2,000.

When asked about his mom and dad's parenting style, he said they were not very supportive and not very involved.

Researchers asked him and his parents questions about his home and family life, and gave him a "risk score".

He scored way above average.

The world isn't giving him much of a chance to live a good life.

>> Histogram: About 90% of kids had a score of 4 or lower. Alex had a risk score of 7.

Over the next 25 years, researchers will interview Alex regularly. He'll be bullied at school. He'll be held back a few grades. He'll be the victim of a violent crime.

Many years from now, when he's an adult, Alex will tell the researchers that he struggles with his physical and mental health.

He'll struggle financially, often living in poverty.

But many people will blame Alex for his circumstances.

Because, when Alex was growing up, US president Ronald Reagan told Americans that poor people are taking advantage of America's generosity. Reagan said people go hungry because of "a lack of knowledge," and that people are homeless "by choice." A few years later, President Clinton will echo the sentiment, saying "personal responsibility" is the way to overcome poverty.

We'll soon learn that most Americans believe the top reason for poverty is drug abuse, and half of Americans believe that poor people aren't doing enough to not be poor.

But, right now, Alex is a teenager. And there's only so much control he has over his life.

He thinks his teachers are pretty good. He likes girls and will soon go on his first date. And his favorite ice cream flavor is vanilla.

In other words, he's like most other teenagers. Let's meet everyone else now.

In this story, we'll follow hundreds of teenagers for the next 24 years, into their late-30s.

They're among the thousands of kids who are part of the National Longitudinal Survey of Youth. This means researchers have followed them since their teenage years to the present day – and beyond.

A notable portion of kids, including Alex, have parents who have an uninvolved parenting style.

These parents "show less warmth" and "display little monitoring of children’s behavior." ¹

¹ Parenting Styles: A Closer Look at a Well-Known Concept by Kuppens and Ceulemans (2018)

A lot of kids are in high-risk environments.

This could mean their home doesn't have basic necessities, like electricity or a quiet place to study. It could also mean their family environment is bad: chaotic routines, parents struggling with disabilities or substance abuse, or unsupportive parents.

The researchers used those questions to calculate a risk score – the higher the score, the more likely it is for kids to have problems.

>> Risk score diagram

And a lot of kids are growing up extremely poor.

Poverty, in and of itself, can be traumatic.

A year from now, in 1998, a researcher named Vincent Felitti will publish a paper that drastically changes the way we think about these kids – and their childhood.

The research will show that these childhood stressors and traumas – called Adverse Childhood Experiences – have a lifelong effect on our health, relationships, happiness, financial security, and pretty much everything else that we value. It will kickstart decades of research that show that our childhood experiences shape our adulthood far more than we ever thought.

While we can't track everything about these kids' lives, we can track the following adverse experiences:

  • Home/family risk score
  • Having uninvolved parents
  • Held back in school
  • School suspensions
  • Being bullied
  • See someone shot with a gun

The home/family risk score is based on 30 questions, so that captures a lot of the environmental factors of a kid's upbringing.

Let's go forward in time. I'll check in at the end of high school.

Keep scrolling ↓

It's 2001 – senior year of high school for a lot of these kids.

They're sorted by the number of adverse experiences they've had.

Here are the kids who grew up in a risky home and family environment.

This is who has been bullied.

And these are the kids who witnessed someone being shot with a gun.

Here, we can see their high school GPA.

These adverse events have a huge impact on whether a kid struggled in school.

High school GPA obviously has a correlation with who attends college the following year.

But so do these adverse childhood events – because, like I said before, these stressors and traumas affect everything about your life.

So that means people who had a bad childhood are much less likely to attend college. They have to figure out how to be an adult without the structure and support that college provides.

Most people on the screen are 18 now – technically adults. But you don't just magically turn into an adult at 18.

Psychologist Jeffrey Arnett argues that, in developed countries, there is era between ages 18 and 25 called "emerging adulthood" when we collectively agree to let people explore the world and figure out what role they want to have in it. For many kids, it will be the first time they have the power to leave their family environment and independently shape their futures.

One of the best places to do that is college. It's a safe, structured, and productive environment for people to experience this era. It allows people to fend off adulthood for a bit longer. And in the last few decades, more people have gone this path.

Americans have embraced emerging adulthood. We've delayed settling down and getting married, further extending this era of freedom and exploration:

Let's check in on Alex.

He's 20 now. The last few years were tough. He was held back in high school, graduated with a 2.9 GPA, and didn't go to college.

But he moved out of his parents' house and he briefly had a job as a grounds maintenance worker. He's not employed now.

It's now 2007. A lot of people now have bachelor's degrees.

But for people who endured many adverse events, a four-year college degree is rare.

Having a bachelor's degree has become much more important in recent decades.

Starting in the 1980s, people without four-year college degrees started earning less and less, while everyone else earned more.

>>> CHART: Median income by educational attainment | Data

Meanwhile, people with college degrees got happier, while people without degrees said they were getting less happy.

>>> CHART: Happiness by degree | Data

College or technical school can mitigate some of the effects of adverse childhood experiences.

But in the last several decades, the cost of college skyrocketed, making college inaccessible for kids who need it most.

>>> CHART: Cost of college | Data CP9 and CP10

In addition, even if everyone had equal access to college, people who experienced more childhood traumas are less likely to finish college.

>>> CHART: ACEs and college completion | Study+data

It's 2008.

In November, the US will elect its first Black president, Barack Obama. About 66% of this generation will vote for him, and they'll think – for a brief moment – that maybe things can get better. He will be less cynical than his predecessor, but he'll also be critiqued for telling Black Americans to take responsibility for improving their lives.

Some people are going to college for the first time in their mid- to late-20s.

In one year, the US will elect Donald Trump as president – a man who constantly insulted poor people and called them "morons."

Most of this generation will vote against him. Most of America will vote against him. He will still win.

It's 2019.

In one year, the world will experience a global pandemic. It will tear through the social and economic stability of virtually every country, decimating our collective mental health. It will kill nearly 1.2 million people in the US.

It's 2021.

They're in their late-30s now, which means they've had plenty of time to shape their own destinies. But the experiences of their childhood still have a huge effect on their lives.

People who struggle financially are far more likely to have experienced adverse events as children.

And it also affects physical health.

People who endure more adverse experiences experience more health problems. Research has found that adverse childhood experiences increase the likelihood of being diagnosed with cancer, heart disease, and mental illness.

Ultimately, it will shorten people's lifespans.

The survey only asks each person about their health every few years, so this visualization shows their most recent response.

How is Alex doing?

He's 37 now, living in the US south. He has a partner and two kids. After decades of working as a cook, he recently moved to a retail job. The last few years, his annual income was around $20,000 and his partner earns about the same amount.

He's struggling with his health – he has been for much of his adult life.

A few years ago, he was asked about his mental health. He said, in the past month, he was depressed some of the time.

This isn't the life the teenager we met 24 years ago thought he would have.

Back in 1997, Alex was 13.

And he was optimistic about the future.

Also, he was skinnier back then.

In fact, most teeangers were optimistic about the future. Even if things weren't perfect, they assumed things would get better.

Yet they were experiencing things that would derail their lives: dysfunctional homes, violence, death, bullies, and more. And they had so little control over these things.

A few years later, they'll turn 18 – and we'll start blaming them for their failures. It'll be their fault for not going to college; their fault for being unhealthy; their fault for not being able to afford housing and healthcare and food.

But these are teenagers. And it's not their fault.
