This is a teenager

by Alvin Chang

This is a teenager.

(We'll call him Alex.)

It's 1997.

Bill Clinton will start his second term as US president. Men In Black and Titanic will be released in theaters. And humans will land a rover on Mars.

But Alex has other things to worry about.

Alex is a 13-year-old Hispanic kid being raised by his dad and step-mom.

Neither parent has a college degree. Their household income is below the poverty line. And their family's net worth is less than $2,000.

When asked about his mom and dad's parenting style, he said they were not very supportive and not very involved.

Researchers asked him and his parents questions about his home and family life, and gave him a "risk score".

He scored way above average.

>> Histogram: About 90% of kids had a score of 4 or lower. Alex had a risk score of 7.

A year from now, in 1998, a researcher named Vincent Felitti will publish a paper that drastically changes the way we think about Alex – and his childhood.

The research will show that these childhood stressors and traumas – called Adverse Childhood Experiences – have a lifelong effect on our health, relationships, future earnings, happiness, and pretty much everything else that we value.

Over the next 25 years, researchers will interview Alex regularly. He'll be bullied at school. He'll be held back a few grades. He'll be the victim of a violent crime.

Many years from now, when he's an adult, Alex will tell the researchers that he struggles with his physical and mental health.

But, right now, Alex is a teenager.

He thinks his teachers are pretty good. He likes girls and will soon go on his first date. And his favorite ice cream flavor is vanilla.

Also, he's pretty optimistic about his future – just like most teenagers.

Let's meet everyone else now.

See!? These teens are very optimistic.

In this story, we'll follow hundreds of teenagers for the next 24 years, nearly into their 40s.

They're among the thousands of kids who are part of the National Longitudinal Survey of Youth, which means researchers have followed them since their teenage years.

A notable portion of kids, including Alex, have parents who display an uninvolved parenting style. These parents "show less warmth" and "display little monitoring of children’s behavior," according to a study that will be published in 20 years.

A lot of kids are growing up in a risky home environment ¹.

Their homes don't feel safe, they don't feel supported, and their day-to-day routine is chaotic. Stress is the norm, and it will erode their physical and mental health.

¹ The survey asked 30 questions that contributed to the home/family risk score.

About 1 in 5 kids are living in poverty.

Kids who grow up in poor households are more likely to experience childhood trauma. Some experts argue that poverty itself is a trauma.

Ultimately, we're about to see many of these teenagers experience terrible things – and there's very little they can do to avoid it. This survey didn't ask about everything, but we can track the following:

  • Uninvolved parents
  • Home/family risk score
  • Family poverty
  • Parents divorcing
  • Held back in school
  • Suspensions from school
  • Being the victim of bullying
  • Being the victim of a violent crime
  • See someone shot with a gun
  • Experiencing homelessness
  • Death of parents, siblings, or partners

Let's keep track of how many of these traumatic experiences each person experiences throughout their lives.

Most of these kids are 13, but as they get older, they'll experience more of these difficult things.

Let's go forward in time.

Most kids are about 15 now – about the age when many of them go on their first date!

OK, enough lovey-dovey stuff.

Back to tracking trauma.

It's 2001 – senior year of high school for a lot of these kids.

So how are their high school grades?

Kids who experience more traumatic events did worse in school.

It's 2002. Most kids are 18 now – technically "adults". And many of them are starting college.

But notice how the kids who go to college are less likely to have experienced difficulties in childhood.

Meanwhile, everyone else has to figure out what being an "adult" looks like.

Kids don't magically turn into adults at 18.

Psychologist Jeffrey Arnett argues that, in developed countries, there is era between ages 18 and 25 called "emerging adulthood" when we collectively agree to let people independently explore the world and figure out what role they want to have in it.

College is a safe, structured, and productive environment for people to experience this era – and in the last few decades, more people have gone this path.

>> CHART: College enrollment | Data

In addition, Americans have delayed settling down and getting married, further extending this era:

>> CHART: Marriage | Data

One thing that is clearly visible after all these years is that a person's race has a huge part to play in how much trauma they experience throughout their life – something Felitti found in his initial study.

Study after study finds Black and Hispanic kids are far more likely to experience an Adverse Childhood Event.

Let's check in on Alex. He's 20 now.

The last few years were tough. He was held back in high school, graduated with a 2.9 GPA, and didn't go to college.

But he moved out of his parents' house and he briefly had a job as a grounds maintenance worker. He's not employed now, but he says he's happy most of the time.

It's 2007, and lots of people now have college degrees.

But, as you can see, people who experienced more childhood trauma are less likely to have a college degree.

Not having a college degree makes your life hard.

Starting in the 1980s, people without college degrees started earning less and less, while everyone else earned more.

>>> CHART: Median income by educational attainment | Data

Meanwhile, people with college degrees got happier, while people without degrees said they were getting less happy.

>>> CHART: Happiness by degree | Data

But also during this period, the cost of college skyrocketed, making college inaccessible for a huge portion of America.

>>> CHART: Cost of college | Data CP9 and CP10

It's 2008.

In November, the US will elect its first Black president, Barack Obama. About 66% will vote for him, and an entire generation will think – for a brief moment – that maybe things can get better.

In one year, the US will elect Donald Trump as president.

Most of this generation will vote against him. Most of America will vote against him. He will still win.

Meanwhile, nearly 200 countries will agree to dramatically cut carbon emissions, in hopes of curbing climate warming. In 10 years, we'll find that these countries are unlikely to meet those goals.

Every few years, the survey asks about mental health, and generally people are happy most of the time.

But people on the higher end of the trauma spectrum are more likely to say they were less frequently happy.

It's 2019.

In one year, the world will experience a global pandemic. It will tear through the social and economic stability of virtually every country, decimating our collective mental health. It will kill nearly 1.2 million people in the US.

It's 2021 – the most recent data we have.

Every few years, the survey asked about people's general health.

We can see that people who experienced more trauma are more likely to struggle with their health. This is what Felitti found in his initial study, and what hundreds of researchers have confirmed since. Childhood traumas have been linked to chronic diseases like cancer, heart disease, and mental illness.

In turn, they were more likely to say that their health limited the amount they could work.

But even without health issues, people higher on the trauma spectrum are more likely to struggle with financial insecurity.

So, how is Alex doing?

He's 37 now, living in the US south. He has a partner and two kids. After decades of working as a cook, he recently moved to a retail job. The last few years, his annual income was around $20,000 and his partner earns about the same amount.

When he was 23, he said he was the victim of a violent crime but he told the interviewer that his life was going OK.

He's in fair health, but he's struggled with being overweight his entire adult life.

In 2019, he was asked about his mental health. He said, some of the time, he's depressed.

There is a remarkable contradiction in the lives of so many kids.

On one hand, they do what kids do – complain about school, go on first dates, hang out with friends, and think about the future. But at the same time, many of them endure dysfunctional homes, witness violence, lose loved ones, struggle in school, and more. They have little control of their environment, yet the evidence hints that our childhood environment has a massive, lifelong impact on our lives.

Yet when they turn 18, we start blaming them for their failures.

It's somehow their fault for not going to college. It's their fault for being unhealthy. It's their fault for not being able to afford housing and healthcare.

But this is a teenager. He is our collective responsibility. They all are.
